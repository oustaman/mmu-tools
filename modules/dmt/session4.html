Quantity now, quality later

Types of Academic Sources

📄 Journal Articles
Research studies published in peer-reviewed journals
Usually 10-30 pages, very specific topics
📚 Books & Book Chapters
Comprehensive coverage of topics
Edited collections offer multiple perspectives
📊 Research Reports
Published by institutions, think tanks, NGOs
Often policy-focused or industry-specific
📰 Conference Papers
Presentations from academic conferences
Often cutting-edge, emerging research

Your Assignment Needs 4 Different Types

Remember: Your final assessment needs 4 sources of different types

Good strategy: Collect at least:

  • • 2-3 journal articles
  • • 1-2 book chapters
  • • 1-2 reports
  • • 1-2 conference papers or other sources

Diversity shows comprehensive research

Primary vs. Secondary Sources

Primary Sources
Original research, first-hand data

Examples: Original research studies, datasets, interviews, experiments, creative works
Secondary Sources
Analysis or interpretation of primary sources

Examples: Literature reviews, textbooks, encyclopedias, critical analyses

Most academic articles are primary sources - that's ideal

Where to Find Journal Articles

1
Academic databases (your best bet)
Academic Search Complete, JSTOR, subject-specific databases
2
Google Scholar (good starting point)
Free, but check if sources are peer-reviewed
3
Citation chaining (expert move)
Check the references of good articles you find

Use the filters: peer-reviewed, last 5-10 years, full-text

Where to Find Books & Chapters

1
Library catalog
Search your university library's collection - physical and e-books
2
Google Books
Preview chapters, check relevance before requesting full book
3
Edited collections
Books with chapters by different authors - great for specific topics

You don't need to read entire books - just relevant chapters

Where to Find Research Reports

Government websites
Departments publish research reports (.gov.uk sites)
Example: Office for National Statistics, Department reports
Think tanks & NGOs
Policy research organizations
Example: Pew Research, World Bank, UNESCO
Industry bodies
Professional associations and trade organizations
Example: Nielsen reports, Gartner, industry white papers

Reports offer real-world data and applications

Quick Quality Checks

Before adding to your collection, quickly check:

  • ✓ Relevance: Does the title/abstract match your topic?
  • ✓ Recency: Is it current enough? (Usually last 5-10 years)
  • ✓ Authority: Who wrote it? Academic or credible institution?
  • ✓ Access: Can you get the full text?
  • ✓ Length: Is it substantial enough? (Not just a 2-page news article)

Skim, don't read deeply yet

How to Read Strategically

1
Read the abstract first
Tells you what the article is about in 150-250 words
2
Skim introduction and conclusion
Get the main argument and findings
3
Look at headings and figures
Understand the structure and key data
4
Deep read only if promising
Save full reading for sources you'll actually use

Don't read everything start-to-finish

Keeping Track of Sources

Create a tracking document
Spreadsheet or table with: Title, Author, Year, Type, Database found, Notes
Save PDFs systematically
Name files clearly: Author_Year_ShortTitle.pdf
Keep them in a dedicated folder for this assignment
Copy full citation immediately
Don't wait - grab citation info when you find the source
You'll need it for your bibliography

Organization now saves time later

Citation Chaining: Finding More Sources

Backward Citation Chaining
Look at the reference list of a good article you found
Find the key sources that informed this research
→ Great for finding foundational work
Forward Citation Chaining
Use Google Scholar's "Cited by" feature
Find newer articles that reference your source
→ Great for finding latest developments

One good source leads to many more

Avoid These Common Mistakes

❌ Using only one source type
You need 4 different types - plan for variety from the start
❌ Sources all too old
Mix of recent (last 5 years) and foundational sources
❌ Not checking access
Make sure you can actually read the full text
❌ Choosing sources just by title
Always read the abstract to verify relevance

Breadth vs. Depth

Breadth sources:
Overview articles, literature reviews, textbook chapters
→ Give you the big picture and common terminology
Depth sources:
Specific research studies, case studies, detailed analyses
→ Provide evidence and detailed arguments

You need both - start broad, then go deep
