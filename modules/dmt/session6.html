Harvard Referencing Mastery

Why Citations Matter

Give credit
Acknowledge others' work and ideas
Avoid plagiarism
Protect yourself academically and legally
Enable verification
Readers can find and check your sources
Show your research
Demonstrate depth and breadth of reading

Citations build credibility and integrity

Two Parts of Every Citation

1. In-Text Citation
Brief reference in your writing
Tells the reader: "This idea came from someone else"

Example: Recent studies show AI is transforming design (Smith, 2023).
2. Reference List / Bibliography
Full details at end of your paper
Tells the reader: "Here's where to find it"

Example: Smith, J. (2023) 'AI in graphic design', Design Studies, 45(2), pp. 123-145.

Both parts must be present for proper citation

Harvard Referencing Style

Author-date system used worldwide

  • • In-text: Author surname and year in parentheses
  • • Reference list: Alphabetical by author surname
  • • Punctuation matters: Commas, full stops, italics all have rules
  • • Different sources, different formats: Books, journals, websites each have their own pattern

Use MMU Cite Them Right Harvard as your guide

In-Text Citations: The Basics

One author:
AI is transforming design practices (Smith, 2023).
Two authors:
Recent research confirms this trend (Smith and Jones, 2023).
Three or more authors:
Multiple studies support this view (Smith et al., 2023).

"et al." means "and others" - use for 3+ authors

In-Text Citations: Author as Part of Sentence

Author in parentheses:
Recent studies show AI is transforming design (Smith, 2023).
Use when focusing on the idea
Author in sentence:
Smith (2023) argues that AI is transforming design.
Use when focusing on the author's contribution
With direct quote:
Smith (2023, p. 45) states that AI "fundamentally changes creative work".
Always include page number for quotes

Citing Multiple Sources

Multiple sources for one point:
Several studies confirm this trend (Smith, 2023; Jones, 2022; Brown, 2021).
Separate with semicolons, order chronologically or alphabetically
Same author, different years:
Smith's research has evolved over time (Smith, 2021, 2022, 2023).
List years chronologically
Same author, same year:
Smith published extensively on this topic (Smith, 2023a, 2023b).
Use a, b, c to distinguish - must match reference list

Reference List: Journal Articles

Standard format:

Author(s) (Year) 'Article title', Journal Name, Volume(Issue), pp. page range.
Example - Print journal:
Smith, J. (2023) 'Artificial intelligence in graphic design', Design Studies, 45(2), pp. 123-145.
Example - Online journal:
Smith, J. (2023) 'Artificial intelligence in graphic design', Design Studies, 45(2), pp. 123-145. Available at: https://doi.org/10.1234/example (Accessed: 15 October 2024).

Note: Article title in 'single quotes', Journal name in italics

Reference List: Books

Standard format:

Author(s) (Year) Book Title. Edition. Place: Publisher.
Example - Whole book:
Jones, P. (2022) Design and Technology. 2nd edn. London: Routledge.
Example - Book chapter:
Brown, L. (2021) 'Digital workflows', in Williams, R. (ed.) Creative Technologies. Manchester: Manchester University Press, pp. 45-67.

Note: Book titles in italics, chapter titles in 'single quotes'

Reference List: Websites & Reports

Website or webpage:
BBC News (2023) AI transforms creative industries. Available at: https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/technology-12345678 (Accessed: 15 October 2024).
Organizational report:
Department for Digital, Culture, Media and Sport (2023) Creative Industries Report 2023. London: HMSO.
PDF report online:
World Economic Forum (2023) Future of Jobs Report 2023. Available at: https://www.weforum.org/reports/future-of-jobs (Accessed: 15 October 2024).

Common Citation Mistakes

❌ Forgetting in-text citations
Every reference list entry must be cited in your text
❌ Wrong punctuation
Commas, full stops, and parentheses all matter - follow the format exactly
❌ Italics vs quotes confusion
Books/journals = italics. Articles/chapters = 'quotes'
❌ No access date for websites
Always include when you accessed online sources
❌ Not alphabetizing reference list
Must be in alphabetical order by author surname

Paraphrasing vs. Direct Quotes

Paraphrasing (preferred):
Put the idea in your own words
Shows you understand the concept
Still needs citation!

Example: Smith (2023) argues that artificial intelligence has fundamentally altered how designers approach their creative work.
Direct quotes (use sparingly):
Copy exact words in "quotation marks"
Use only when the exact wording matters
Must include page number

Example: Smith (2023, p. 45) states that AI "fundamentally changes the nature of creative work".

Aim for 80% paraphrasing, 20% quotes

2
Arrange alphabetically

3
Use hanging indent
First line flush left, subsequent lines indented
4
Double-check every entry
Punctuation, italics, spelling
