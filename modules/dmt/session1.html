Real-World Example: 36 Days of Type

What it is: Annual typography challenge where thousands of designers worldwide create their own version of the same letter or number each day for 36 days

Why this matters for your research:

Same brief (design the letter "A"), but every designer approaches it differently - minimalist, 3D, animated, illustrative, abstract. This is exactly what happens with research topics!

Key Takeaways:

  • ‚Ä¢ Your topic choice is just the starting point - your angle makes it unique
  • ‚Ä¢ Different perspectives on the same subject all have value
  • ‚Ä¢ Creativity works within constraints (you can be original even with common topics)

Find it: Instagram #36daysoftype | Behance | 36daysoftype.com

Real-World Example: Daily UI Challenge

What it is: 100-day challenge where designers receive the same UI prompt (like "design a sign-up page") and create their interpretation. Over 500,000 designers, 20+ million different solutions

Why this matters for your research:

Everyone starts with "design a checkout page" but some focus on accessibility, others on mobile, others on e-commerce vs. banking. The research question you ask determines what you find.

Key Takeaways:

  • ‚Ä¢ The questions you ask shape the research you do
  • ‚Ä¢ Narrowing your focus makes your work stronger (not weaker)
  • ‚Ä¢ Two people researching "AI in design" can produce completely different portfolios

Find it: collectui.com | Dribbble #dailyui | dailyui.co

Real-World Example: Same Event, Different Coverage

What it is: The same tech event (Apple launch, Meta rebrand, TikTok regulation) covered by The Guardian, BBC, TechCrunch, The Verge, Financial Times - same story, completely different angles and emphasis

Why this matters for your research:

Each outlet asks different questions: "What's the business impact?" vs "How does this affect users?" vs "What's the technical innovation?" Different databases and sources give you different perspectives - just like news outlets.

Key Takeaways:

  • ‚Ä¢ Where you look determines what you find (databases = different perspectives)
  • ‚Ä¢ Your audience shapes how you present information
  • ‚Ä¢ Multiple sources give you a fuller picture than a single source

Try it: BBC News | TechCrunch | The Guardian | The Verge

From Social Media to Academic Standards

The examples we just looked at are mostly informal social media - creative, inspiring, but largely unregulated.

At university, we do things properly:

  • ‚Ä¢ Due process - systematic research methods
  • ‚Ä¢ Ethical standards - proper attribution, avoiding plagiarism
  • ‚Ä¢ Library resources - catalog, databases, and expert support at the center of your work
  • ‚Ä¢ Academic rigor - even when studying informal phenomena like TikTok or memes

This is a journey:

You won't learn everything in a single session. But by the time you graduate, you'll be far more systematic and structured in how you think and how you work - whether you're analyzing social media trends or developing new technologies.
