The Mistake Everyone Makes

Jumping Straight to Solutions

"The app needs a chatbot!"
"We should add more colors!"
"Make it like Instagram!"

The Right Way

First understand the problem.
Then solve it.

Good UX starts with understanding what's actually wrong

Real Example: Nokia's Mistake

The Situation (2007):

Nokia was the world's largest phone manufacturer. iPhone launched.

What Nokia thought the problem was:

"We need better hardware. More features. Faster processors."

What the real problem was:

"People want simple, intuitive software. Touch interfaces. Apps that just work."

Result: Nokia went from market leader to almost bankrupt in 5 years

Symptoms vs. Root Problems

ü§í Symptoms (What you see)

  • "Users don't click the button"
  • "People abandon the checkout"
  • "No one uses this feature"
  • "Complaints about the app"

üéØ Root Problems (Why it happens)

  • "Button isn't visible enough"
  • "Checkout has too many steps"
  • "Feature doesn't solve user needs"
  • "App is too complicated"

Always dig deeper than the surface symptom

The 5 Whys Technique

A simple method from Toyota to find root causes: Keep asking "Why?" until you find the real problem

Problem: Users abandon the library booking app
Why? They can't find available study rooms
Why? The calendar view is confusing
Why? It shows ALL rooms, not just available ones
Why? No filter for "available now"
Root cause: Missing a simple availability filter

Practice: 5 Whys

  • 1. Ask "Why?" to find deeper reasons
  • 2. Keep going until you find the root cause
  • 3. Write down your chain of whys

Asking Good Research Questions

‚ùå Bad Questions

  • "Do you like this app?"
    ‚Üí Too vague
  • "Would you use a chatbot?"
    ‚Üí Leading question
  • "What features do you want?"
    ‚Üí Users aren't designers

‚úÖ Good Questions

  • "What's frustrating about...?"
    ‚Üí Uncovers pain points
  • "Tell me about last time you..."
    ‚Üí Gets real stories
  • "What prevents you from...?"
    ‚Üí Finds barriers

Writing a Problem Statement

Format:

[User group] needs a way to [user need] because [insight/problem]

Example 1: Students need a way to find available study spaces quickly because the current booking system shows all rooms regardless of availability, forcing them to check each room individually.

Example 2: Local residents need a way to report potholes easily because the council website requires login, multiple forms, and doesn't provide status updates, leading to unreported issues.

This becomes part of Section 3 in your slide deck

Case Study: Airbnb's Research

Early problem: Listings weren't getting bookings

What they thought:

"Need better marketing, more features, lower prices"

What research revealed:

"Listings with professional photos get 2-3x more bookings"

The real problem:

Poor quality photos made listings look unappealing and untrustworthy

Solution: Free professional photography service ‚Üí Bookings doubled

Defining problems as solutions

‚Üí "Needs a chatbot" is a solution, not a problem

‚Üí Show data, quotes, observations
