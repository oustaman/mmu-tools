Part 1: Prototype

Hand-drawn sketches (6-10 screens) + User flow diagram

Component A: Hand-Drawn Screen Sketches

Requirements Checklist:

üí° Pro Tips:

  • ‚Ä¢ Draw on paper, whiteboard, or tablet - but keep it hand-drawn (shows thinking process)
  • ‚Ä¢ Include labels/annotations showing where your 2 UCD principles appear
  • ‚Ä¢ Take photos in good lighting - blur = lost marks
  • ‚Ä¢ Don't worry about artistic skill - focus on clarity of UX thinking
  • ‚Ä¢ Show different states (e.g., empty state, error state, success state)

Component B: User Flow Diagram (MANDATORY)

Flow Diagram Checklist:

‚úì What Makes a Good Flow Diagram:

  • ‚Ä¢ Shows the complete user journey from start to finish
  • ‚Ä¢ Decision points ask clear questions ("Room available?" ‚Üí Yes/No paths)
  • ‚Ä¢ Includes error handling ("What if payment fails?")
  • ‚Ä¢ Easy to follow - someone else could trace the path
  • ‚Ä¢ Demonstrates your understanding of information architecture

‚úó Common Mistakes:

  • ‚Ä¢ Just showing screen order without decisions (that's a sitemap, not a flow)
  • ‚Ä¢ Missing error states or alternative paths
  • ‚Ä¢ No clear entry or exit points
  • ‚Ä¢ So complex it's unreadable - simplify!
  • ‚Ä¢ Forgetting to include it in Section 4 of slide deck

2-3 slides, ~300 words total

Section 1 Checklist:

Slide 1: Service Overview (150-200 words)

This slide should cover:

  • ‚Ä¢ Description of the community service/small business you selected
  • ‚Ä¢ Current service delivery method (if applicable)
  • ‚Ä¢ Why this service needs UX improvement (brief overview)
  • ‚Ä¢ Context: social/community importance
‚úì Strong Example

"I selected the Manchester Central Library's study room booking system. Currently, students must visit the library website, navigate through multiple menus, and complete a lengthy form to book study spaces. The system is desktop-only, has no real-time availability display, and frequently times out during peak periods. This creates significant friction for students who need quiet study spaces, particularly during exam season when rooms are in highest demand. The library serves over 15,000 students annually and study room bookings have decreased 40% since 2020, despite increased student enrollment. Improving this experience would directly support student academic success and better utilize library resources."

Slide 2: User Analysis (150-200 words)

This slide should cover:

  • ‚Ä¢ Target user group/audience definition
  • ‚Ä¢ User demographics and characteristics
  • ‚Ä¢ User needs, goals, and pain points
  • ‚Ä¢ Context of use (where, when, why users need this service)
‚úì Strong Example

"The primary users are Manchester Met students aged 18-25, studying across all disciplines but particularly concentrated in exam-heavy courses like Business, Law, and Sciences. These users are digitally native, expect mobile-first experiences, and frequently multitask between study sessions. Their main goal is to quickly secure quiet study spaces during peak periods (9am-5pm weekdays, especially weeks 10-12 of semester). Key pain points include: inability to check availability on-the-go, lengthy booking process requiring desktop access, no confirmation notifications, and difficulty canceling bookings when plans change. Users report abandoning bookings and studying in less suitable environments (cafes, accommodation) due to system friction. Context: These bookings typically happen on-the-move between lectures, in 2-3 minute windows when users spot a gap in their schedule. The current desktop-only system doesn't match this usage pattern, creating a fundamental mismatch between user needs and system design."

Word count: ~175 words ‚úì

Section 2 Checklist:

Slide 6: User-Centered Design Principles (200-250 words) ‚≠ê KEY SLIDE

This is THE most important slide in Section 2. You must:

  • ‚Ä¢ Name 2 UCD principles you applied in your design
  • ‚Ä¢ Show specific examples from your sketches where each principle appears
  • ‚Ä¢ Explain the rationale - why these principles matter for your users
‚úì Excellent Example

"I applied two key user-centered design principles throughout my prototype: Visibility of System Status and Error Prevention. Visibility of System Status: On the booking screen (Sketch 3), I included a real-time availability indicator showing room status with color-coding: green for 'Available Now', amber for 'Available in 30min', and red for 'Fully Booked Today'. A countdown timer shows 'Holding your selection: 2:45 remaining' during the booking process. This addresses users' primary pain point of not knowing whether rooms are truly available. When users can see system status clearly, they make confident decisions and experience less anxiety during time-sensitive tasks like booking study rooms during peak periods. Error Prevention: Rather than allowing users to make invalid bookings, the interface prevents errors before they occur. Sketch 5 shows time-slot selection where past times are automatically greyed out and unselectable. If a user tries to book a 4-hour slot but only 2 hours remain before closing, the system constrains the selection and displays: 'Maximum 2 hours available today'. This prevents the frustration of completing a booking only to receive an error message. For students booking between lectures, preventing errors is more efficient than fixing them later."

Why this works:

  • ‚úì Names 2 specific principles with clear headings
  • ‚úì References specific sketches ("Sketch 3", "Sketch 5")
  • ‚úì Explains HOW each principle appears in the design
  • ‚úì Connects principles to user needs and pain points
  • ‚úì ~230 words - within range
‚úó Weak Example

"I used good UX principles. My design is user-friendly and accessible. I made sure everything is easy to find and use. The interface is clean and simple which is important for users."

Problems:

  • ‚úó Doesn't name 2 specific principles
  • ‚úó No connection to sketches - no examples shown
  • ‚úó Vague claims ("user-friendly", "clean") without evidence
  • ‚úó No rationale for why principles matter
  • ‚úó Too short (~40 words, needs 200-250)

Section 3: Evaluation & Recommendations

3-4 slides, ~500 words total

‚ñº

Section 3 Checklist:

Slide 9: Problem Analysis (200-250 words)

For services being enhanced OR new services, explain:

  • ‚Ä¢ What problems existed before (or what users had to do without this service)
  • ‚Ä¢ Evidence of these problems (user complaints, inefficiencies, data, observations)
  • ‚Ä¢ Why these problems mattered to users (impact)
  • ‚Ä¢ Context and scale of the problem
‚úì Strong Example with Evidence

"The existing Manchester Central Library study room booking system created three critical problems for students. Problem 1: Hidden availability. Students had no way to check room status without visiting the website and navigating through five separate pages. User interviews revealed students wasted an average of 12 minutes per booking attempt, with 40% abandoning the process. Problem 2: Desktop-only access. The system required desktop browsers; 78% of students reported needing to book 'on-the-go' between lectures using mobile devices, forcing them to find computer labs or wait until reaching home‚Äîby which time rooms were taken. Problem 3: No booking feedback. After submitting a booking request, students received no confirmation notification and had to manually check their email (often missing confirmations in spam). Library data showed 23% of booked rooms went unused because students forgot or weren't certain their booking succeeded. These problems particularly impacted students during exam periods when study spaces are most critical, directly affecting their ability to prepare effectively."

Word count: ~185 words ‚úì | Evidence included: User interviews, usage data, percentages

Slide 10: Solution Evaluation (200-250 words)

Explain how your design solves the problems:

  • ‚Ä¢ How your design addresses each identified problem
  • ‚Ä¢ Specific features or design decisions that solve user pain points
  • ‚Ä¢ Expected improvements in user experience
  • ‚Ä¢ How you would measure success
‚úì Strong Solution Evaluation

"My mobile-first booking app directly addresses each identified problem. Solution to Problem 1: The home screen displays all study rooms with real-time color-coded availability (green/amber/red), allowing students to see status instantly without navigation. Expected improvement: Reduce decision time from 12 minutes to under 30 seconds. Solution to Problem 2: Mobile-native design with offline capability enables booking anywhere, anytime. Students can bookmark favorite rooms, see availability between lectures, and complete bookings in 3 taps. Expected improvement: Increase booking completion rate from 60% to 95%. Solution to Problem 3: Push notifications confirm bookings immediately and send reminders 1 hour before scheduled time. QR code check-in at room entrance validates booking and releases unused rooms automatically. Expected improvement: Reduce unused bookings from 23% to under 5%. Success metrics: (1) Average booking time, (2) Booking completion rate, (3) Room utilization rate, (4) User satisfaction scores. These measurements would be tracked through app analytics and quarterly user surveys."

Word count: ~180 words ‚úì | Directly maps solutions to problems with measurable outcomes

Slide 11: Recommendations (150-200 words)

Professional recommendations should be:

  • ‚Ä¢ Specific: Not "make it better" but concrete actions
  • ‚Ä¢ Actionable: Realistic steps that can actually be implemented
  • ‚Ä¢ Prioritized: What should happen first, second, third
  • ‚Ä¢ Forward-thinking: Next iteration, additional testing, future features
‚úì Strong Recommendations

"To successfully implement this booking system, I recommend a phased approach. Phase 1 (Immediate): Conduct usability testing with 10-15 students using paper prototypes to validate the 3-tap booking flow and identify any navigation issues before development. Phase 2 (Development): Build MVP focusing on core features: room availability display, mobile booking, and push notifications. Integrate with existing library database via API. Phase 3 (Testing): Beta test with 50 students over 2 weeks during low-demand period, monitoring booking completion rates and gathering feedback through in-app surveys. Future Enhancements: Group booking functionality for study groups, favorite room lists, booking history, and integration with student timetables to suggest optimal study times. Scalability: The design framework could extend to other campus resources (equipment bookings, seminar rooms) if successful. Success depends on strong collaboration with library IT team and maintaining backup manual booking process during transition period."

Word count: ~165 words ‚úì | Specific phases, actionable steps, considers implementation
