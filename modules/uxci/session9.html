Critical Evaluation Framework

Ask yourself honestly:

  • ❓Does your design actually solve the problem?
  • 📊How do you know it works?
  • 📝What's your evidence?
  • ⚖️What trade-offs did you make?

Problem Analysis: What to Include

200-250 words covering:

  • 1.What problems existed: Specific issues users faced
  • 2.Evidence: Data, quotes, observations proving these problems
  • 3.Why it mattered: Impact on users (time, frustration, task failure)
  • 4.Context: When/where these problems occurred

This is either "enhancing existing experience" OR "creating new experience"

Library Study Room Booking (234 words)

Students at MMU struggled to find and book available study rooms efficiently. The current booking system required users to navigate through multiple menus—clicking from the library homepage to services, then facilities, then booking—taking an average of 7 clicks navigation depth and 3 minutes time-on-task to reach the availability calendar. User reviews on app stores showed consistent complaints: "By the time I found a room, it was already taken" and "Too slow, just gave up." user quotes

Analytics data revealed a 73% abandonment rate key metric on the booking flow, with most users leaving after the second screen. Observational research showed students checking multiple times throughout the day, wasting 10–15 minutes observed behaviour between lectures trying to secure study space. During exam periods, this problem intensified, with booking times extending to 5+ minutes peak scenario due to high demand and limited availability information.

This inefficiency directly impacted students' academic performance and stress levels. Lost study time meant less preparation for assessments, while the frustration of the booking process discouraged library use entirely. Students reported "choosing cafés or home just to avoid the system" user behaviour — undermining the library's role as a primary learning space and creating measurable drop-off in facility utilisation downstream impact.

Library Study Room Booking Solution (221 words)

My design addresses the core availability problem problem solved by displaying all free study rooms directly on the home screen, using color-coding (green=available now, yellow=available soon, grey=booked). This eliminates the 7-click navigation process entirely. Users can see room status, capacity, and equipment at a glance, applying filters without leaving the main view.

The booking flow is reduced to two steps simplified flow: select room and confirm time. Real-time updates prevent the "already taken" frustration quote resolved by showing live availability as other students book. Error prevention is built in through availability constraints — users cannot select unavailable slots, and the system confirms bookings immediately.

Expected improvements: booking time from 3 minutes → ~30 seconds 6× faster, abandonment rates from 73% → under 30% key metric, navigation confusion eliminated entirely. Success measured through: completion rates, time-on-task, satisfaction surveys, support requests success metrics. Design applies Heuristic #1 (visibility of system status) and Heuristic #5 (error prevention) heuristics applied, directly addressing usability issues found in research.

The Same Pattern Everywhere

Problem → Solution → Impact. This pattern works in assignments, portfolios, job applications — anywhere you need to show you understand a problem and can fix it.

Cover letter extract — UX Designer (graduate)

During my final-year project, I identified that students at my university were spending an average of 7 clicks and 3 minutes just to book a study room problem — a friction point confirmed by a 73% task-abandonment rate key metric I uncovered through analytics review and user interviews. I redesigned the booking flow to surface room availability on the home screen with real-time colour-coding, reducing the process to two taps solution and eliminating the navigational dead-ends that caused users to give up. Projected outcome testing showed a 6× reduction in booking time and an expected drop in abandonment to under 30% impact — demonstrating that clearly visible system status and error prevention aren't just heuristic principles, but measurable design decisions.

💡

Problem → Solution → Impact is the core narrative structure for documenting UX work — in your assignment write-up, your portfolio case study, and your job application cover letter. Recruiters and markers both look for the same thing: did you understand the problem, what did you do about it, and how do you know it worked?

Evidence-Based Claims

Don't just say "it's better" - explain WHY

❌ Weak Claims:

  • • "My design is better"
  • • "Users will like it"
  • • "It's more intuitive"
  • • "Solves all problems"

✅ Strong Claims:

  • • "Reduces clicks from 7 to 2"
  • • "Shows status immediately (Heuristic 1)"
  • • "Prevents booking errors"
  • • "Testing revealed 3 major improvements"

Recommendations: What to Include

150-200 words covering:

  • 1.Next steps: What should be developed next?
  • 2.Additional testing: What needs validation?
  • 3.Future features: What could be added later?
  • 4.Implementation: Realistic considerations
  • 5.Scalability: How could this grow?

What Makes Good Recommendations?

Specific

Not "improve it" but "add push notifications for booking confirmations"

Actionable

Something that can actually be done with resources available

Realistic

Acknowledges constraints (time, budget, technical limitations)

Prioritized

What's most important to do first?
