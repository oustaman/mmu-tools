What is Google Stitch?

A free AI tool from Google Labs that turns a text prompt — or a sketch photo — into a high-fidelity mobile or web UI in seconds

🌐stitch.withgoogle.com — free, just needs a Google account
✍️Describe your screen in plain English — or upload your paper sketch
🎨Generates multiple screen variants with real components, typography, colour
📤Export to Figma, copy as HTML/CSS, or screenshot for your slide deck
🔗New canvas mode: stitch screens together into interactive prototype flows

What Stitch Is (and Isn't) Good For

✅ Great for

Generating a starting point fast
Turning paper sketches into digital screens
Exploring multiple visual directions quickly
Non-designers producing credible-looking UI

⚠️ Limitations

Doesn't think about your users — you do
One screen at a time, not a full flow
Complex interactions still need human design
Output needs review against your UCD principles

💡 Think of Stitch as a fast sketching tool — it gives you raw material, not a finished design

Activity: Try Stitch (10 min)

Generate one key screen from your community service app

1. Go to stitch.withgoogle.com and sign in
2. Write a prompt — include: app purpose, screen name, key elements (e.g. "a home screen for a local library app showing search, opening hours, and event listings, clean and accessible")
3. Try a second variant with a different style or layout
4. Screenshot or export the version closest to your vision

🎯 Goal: a usable screenshot to drop into your prototype later today

Stitch Problem: Inconsistent Navbars

⚠️ Even when you ask Stitch to generate multiple screens for the same app, the navbar or bottom menu often shifts — different icons, labels, or layout. This breaks the illusion of a real product.

Solution 1 — Prompt discipline

Be pedantic and persistent in your prompts

  • • Lock the navbar in your prompt every time: "keep the same bottom nav with exactly these 4 tabs: Home, Search, Book, Profile"
  • • Describe icon labels, order, and active state explicitly
  • • If it drifts, regenerate — don't accept a broken version

Takes patience but gives you the cleanest, most consistent output

Solution 2 — Crop and overlay

Fix inconsistency in your slide deck directly

  • • From your best-looking screen, crop just the navbar
  • • On every other slide, cover the wrong navbar by placing your cropped version on top as an image overlay
  • • In Google Slides or PowerPoint: paste image → drag to position → align to bottom

Faster fix — good enough for a prototype, especially if you're short on time

Screens vs Flows — What's the Difference?

🖼 A screen

A static picture of one state of the UI

  • • Shows layout and content
  • • Has no memory of where the user came from
  • • Has no awareness of what happens next

Alone, it cannot be tested or evaluated as a user experience

🔗 A flow

A sequence of connected screens that tells a story

  • • Represents a task from start to finish
  • • Shows decisions and paths
  • • Reveals transitions, errors, and confirmations

This is what you prototype and test

Thinking in Flows: The Mindset Shift

Stop asking "what does this screen look like?" — start asking:

👤 Who is the user and what are they trying to accomplish? One task at a time.
🚪 Where do they enter? What is the first screen they see, and why are they here?
➡️ What triggers the next screen? A button tap, a form submit, a selection?
❌ What can go wrong? What does the user see if they make an error or tap the wrong thing?
🏁 What does success look like? What is the final confirmation or destination?

How to Map Your Flow Before You Build It

Do this on paper or in your head first — before touching any tool

1 Pick one task — e.g. "Book a library seat" or "Report a missed bin collection"
2 List every screen the user needs to pass through to complete that task (aim for 4–7)
3 Name the trigger between each screen — what does the user tap, type, or select?
4 Mark decision points — where the flow branches (e.g. logged in / not logged in)
5 Now build your screens — one slide (or Stitch export) per screen in your flow

Activity: Map Your Flow (10 min)

Pick your most important user task and map the screens

Draw on paper:

  • • Boxes = screens (give each a name)
  • • Arrows = user actions (label them)
  • • Diamonds = decision points
  • • Aim for a happy path of 4–7 screens

Ask yourself:

  • • Does every screen have a clear purpose?
  • • Can the user go back at every point?
  • • What's the very last screen they see?
  • • Are there any screens you're missing?

✓ This map becomes the blueprint for your prototype — each box is one slide

Building a Clickable Prototype in Google Slides or PowerPoint

Google Slides

1. One slide = one screen. Paste your Stitch screenshot or sketch as a background image.
2. Draw a transparent shape over a button area: Insert → Shape → Rectangle. Set fill to transparent, no border.
3. Right-click the shape → Link → Slides in this presentation → pick the destination slide.
4. Repeat for every tappable element. Present in Slideshow mode — clicking the hotspot jumps to the linked slide.

PowerPoint

1. One slide = one screen. Paste your screen design as an image.
2. Draw a shape over the button area. In Format Shape: fill = No Fill, line = No Line.
3. Right-click shape → Hyperlink → Place in This Document → choose the slide.
4. Run Slide Show → From Beginning. Click hotspots to navigate. Share the .pptx or a published link.

💡 Tip: Add a "back" hotspot on every screen so testers can always navigate backwards

Figma vs Slide Deck — Which Should You Use?

Figma

  • ✅ Industry-standard design tool
  • ✅ Precise components and constraints
  • ✅ Native prototyping with transitions
  • ✅ Great for building a design portfolio
  • ⏱ Learning curve if you're not already familiar
  • ⏱ Free tier limitations on projects

Google Slides / PowerPoint

  • ✅ Tools you already know
  • ✅ Fast to set up — no onboarding
  • ✅ Easy to share a link or file
  • ✅ Perfectly adequate for 4–8 screen flows
  • ⚠ Less precise than Figma for spacing/layout
  • ⚠ No component reuse or design systems
For this assessment: no preference, no extra marks for using Figma over a slide deck. What matters is that your prototype demonstrates your design decisions and is clickable.

What Your Prototype Needs to Show

From the brief — your prototype must demonstrate two key UCD principles

It must be clickable — a single static screen is not a prototype. The assessor needs to navigate through it.
It must represent a real user task — show a complete flow, not just your best-looking screen.
It must reflect your design decisions — the choices you discuss in Section 2 of your slide deck should be visible in the prototype.
Submit a link — a shared Google Slides link, a published Figma prototype URL, or a shared PowerPoint link. Test the link before submitting.
